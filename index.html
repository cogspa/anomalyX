A Manifesto

Most studios generate content. We build worlds.

The current era of AI production is dominated by prompts. A user types, a system generates, an output appears, and the chain ends. The artifact has no spatial memory, no reusability, no relationship to the world it pretends to depict. It is content, not a world.

Anomaly X Productions exists in opposition to that paradigm. We do not generate single shots; we author reconstructable environments — worlds that can be navigated, re-lit, re-performed, and re-deployed across film, XR, installation, and simulation. Every scene we produce is a system that can be revisited.

The "X" in our name marks our commitment to Authored Anomalies™ — the deliberate, narrative-driven irregularities that we embed into every world. Where competitors aim for generic photorealism, we treat the anomaly as the signal. The break in logic, the impossible shadow, the scale distortion. These are not glitches. They are the fingerprints of authorship.

This site documents how we work, why our pipeline is structured the way it is, and what makes it different from anything else operating in this space.

The Problem with Current Pipelines

Traditional virtual production and AI content pipelines suffer from the same fundamental constraint: they are linear, asset-based, and built around a one-way flow of creation to render. Asset-heavy workflows require expensive modeling for every scene variation. AI content pipelines generate output but produce nothing reusable — every prompt is a dead end. Compositing workflows layer footage on top of environments, never inside them. And real-time engines are treated as the place where worlds are built, not where they are activated. The result: fragile, one-off productions with no spatial memory and no system-level reusability. Anomaly X Productions was founded to address this directly.

The Authored Reality Pipeline™

The Authored Reality Pipeline™ is the studio's core production methodology. It replaces the traditional linear chain — model → texture → light → render — with a closed-loop system: Author → Capture → Encode → Activate. Worlds are designed procedurally, intersected with live-action performance, encoded as perceptual data via Gaussian splats, and deployed in real-time engines as reconstructable environments.

The Five Stages

01 — Sketch · Luma's Innovative Dreams. Hybrid concepting and previs. Dream Machine and Ray3 ideate the world's tone, motion language, and visual references before structure is committed.

02 — Generate · Marble World Builder. Environments generated through rule-based systems, not handcrafted geometry. Spatial logic, scale relationships, and narrative intent embedded at the foundation. Every world receives at least one Authored Anomaly™ — a designed irregularity that gives the environment its identity.

03 — Composite · Beeble. Live-action footage processed through Beeble's AI-driven keying and relighting. Performers are not composited onto environments — they are integrated into them, with lighting and color matched to the procedural world's logic.

04 — Convert · Postshot. Unified scenes converted into Gaussian splat representations. This stage is governed by the studio's proprietary Perceptual Encoding Strategy (PES), which prioritizes visual hierarchy, focal density, and motion readability over raw geometric fidelity.

05 — Activate · Unreal Engine. Encoded environments deployed into Unreal for cinematic output, interactive experience, XR delivery, or installation. Critically, Unreal is not where worlds are built — it is where authored worlds are played back.

Worldwright™ — The Orchestration System

Worldwright™ is the proprietary orchestration system that automates and unifies the Authored Reality Pipeline™. It is the connective tissue between every tool in the chain — and the system of record for every world the studio produces.

It standardizes project structure across every stage, tracks assets, versions, and transformations through the entire pipeline, automates handoffs between procedural generation, compositing, splat conversion, and real-time deployment, encodes scenes as structured data, and maintains world memory — every environment becomes a reusable, reconstructable system.

At its foundation is SceneDNA™, the studio's proprietary metadata and scripting schema for describing world behavior. SceneDNA encodes environment logic (gravity, scale, architectural rules), spatial and narrative relationships, performance layers, splat prioritization and density mapping, real-time deployment configuration, and the Authored Anomalies registry.

"SceneDNA™ allows worlds to be described not just visually, but structurally — bridging creative intent with computational systems."

White Paper II Software Stack April 2026

The Authored Reality Pipeline — tool by tool.

How Marble, Beeble, Postshot, and Unreal Engine combine — under Worldwright orchestration — to produce reconstructable virtual worlds.

Why This Stack

The Authored Reality Pipeline™ is a deliberate composition. Each of its primary tools represents the current state of the art in its category, and each was selected for what it produces for the next stage, not for its standalone capability. The pipeline is designed around handoffs.

Two principles guided tool selection. First, each tool must output a format that the next tool consumes natively — no glue scripts, no manual conversion bottlenecks. Second, each tool must support local or controlled execution. For client confidentiality, IP protection, and production reliability, the studio prioritizes tools that can be operated on-premises or with full data control.

Stage 02 — Marble World Builder

Vendor: World Labs (founded by Fei-Fei Li). Role: Procedural environment generation. Output: Gaussian splats, triangle meshes, or video.

Marble is a generative multimodal world model. From a text prompt, image, video clip, panorama, or coarse 3D layout, Marble produces a persistent, navigable 3D environment that can be explored without the morphing or temporal inconsistency that affects frame-by-frame generative systems. Marble 1.1 (the current release) prioritizes lighting fidelity and reduced artifacts.

Capabilities the studio uses

  • Multimodal input: Text, image, multi-image (up to 8), video, panoramas, or coarse 3D layouts via the Chisel tool, which separates structure from style.
  • Persistent geometry: Worlds are stable assets, not real-time hallucinations. They can be revisited and integrated downstream without drift.
  • Editing operations: Local edits, large-scale structural changes, and the Expand and Compose features for stitching multiple worlds into larger continuous environments.
  • Export formats: Gaussian splat, triangle mesh (low-poly for physics, high-poly for visuals), and rendered video.

The Chisel layout tool enables a critical workflow for Anomaly X: defining structural rules before generation. This is where Authored Anomalies™ are introduced. Rather than accepting whatever the model produces, the artist constrains the world's geometry first, then introduces a deliberate irregularity into the structural prompt. The anomaly survives generation because it was authored before it.

Stage 03 — Beeble (SwitchLight 3.0)

Vendor: Beeble. Role: AI-driven keying, PBR extraction, and relighting. Output: Multi-channel 16-bit EXR with normals, base color, roughness, metallic, specular, alpha, and depth.

Beeble's SwitchLight is a video-to-PBR neural model. SwitchLight 3.0 processes multiple frames simultaneously for temporal stability, replacing the per-frame plus deflicker approach of version 2.0. The model is trained on roughly ten times the data of its predecessor and produces fewer artifacts on fine detail like hair, skin, and fabric.

The studio runs Beeble Studio, the desktop version, which executes locally on GPU hardware. This is non-negotiable for client work involving talent likeness, NDA-bound projects, or any footage that cannot be cloud-uploaded.

Capabilities the studio uses

  • Full-scene PBR generation — passes for actors, props, vehicles, architecture, environments. Used for shots that include practical set pieces alongside performers.
  • 4K, unlimited duration — up to one hour at full precision, locally on GPU.
  • 16-bit EXR output — multi-channel files containing all AOV passes in a single deliverable.
  • Beeble Editor — 3D editing environment for previewing relighting in real time, with HDRI lighting and direct import of GLTF, splats, HDRIs, and background plates.
  • Render queue + native plugins for Blender, Unreal Engine, and Nuke.

The technical key here is that SwitchLight produces PBR passes, not just a keyed alpha. A performer captured in standard studio lighting can be re-lit in post to match the warm sunset of a Marble cathedral, the cold blue of an underwater scene, or the harsh top-down light of an authored anomaly that violates real-world physics. The anomaly's lighting is in the world; Beeble brings the performer into it.

Stage 04 — Postshot

Vendor: Jawset. Role: Gaussian splat reconstruction and scene encoding. Output: PLY (Gaussian splat radiance field) and Postshot's native .psht.

Postshot is a desktop application for Gaussian splat reconstruction — now considered the leading desktop platform in its category. It accepts photographs, image sequences, or video, performs camera tracking and alignment, and trains splat representations of the captured scene.

Capabilities the studio uses

  • Multiple training profiles — Splat3 (default, fine-detail focus), Splat MCMC (more forgiving on imperfect captures), and Splat ADC (legacy growth-rate-controlled).
  • Splat count control — hard limits on Gaussian primitives, allowing the studio to tune for memory, render performance, and detail level. Essential for Unreal deployment.
  • Live training preview — quality monitored in real time, parameters adjustable mid-run.
  • RealityCapture import — alignment data from RealityCapture produces faster and more reliable poses than Postshot's built-in tracking. Studio standard for capture-heavy shots.
  • Sky model — projects environment data onto a sphere, reducing the floating-cloud artifacts that plague outdoor splat reconstructions.
  • Region-of-interest learning, masking, editing — cleanup tools for artifact removal and focal prioritization.

Postshot is where the pipeline performs its Perceptual Translation. The studio's Perceptual Encoding Strategy (PES) governs this stage — specifying density priority for narrative focal zones (faces, anomalies, hero objects), splat count budgets matched to the target Unreal deployment, profile selection based on capture quality, sky model usage, and region masking. PES is the difference between running a tool and operating it with intent.

Stage 05 — Unreal Engine

Vendor: Epic Games. Role: Real-time activation, camera control, interaction, and final output.

Unreal Engine is the de facto standard for virtual production. The studio uses Unreal for Gaussian splat rendering (via XVERSE 3D-GS, Postshot's plugin, and evolving native support), real-time camera systems for previs and final-pixel work, Sequencer and Movie Render Queue for cinematic output, Niagara for VFX integration with imported splat scenes, Lumen and Nanite for conventional CG supplements, and multi-platform deployment to cinematic, interactive, XR, and installation targets.

Why it's stage five — and not stage one

This is the philosophical inversion at the heart of the Authored Reality Pipeline. In conventional virtual production, Unreal is the studio. Worlds are built in Unreal. Lighting is set in Unreal. Cameras are blocked in Unreal.

In our pipeline, Unreal is the Activation Layer. The world has already been authored (Marble), the performance has already been integrated (Beeble), and the scene has already been encoded (Postshot). What enters Unreal is a finished perceptual artifact that the engine plays back and makes interactive. Iteration on environment design happens in Marble, lighting iteration happens in Beeble, splat budget iteration happens in Postshot. Unreal becomes a deployment and interaction layer — fast, focused, and free to handle the things real-time engines do best.

Worldwright — The Orchestration Layer

Each tool above produces an artifact. Each must be tracked, named, versioned, and routed to the next stage. Doing this manually across a real production schedule — multiple shots, revision rounds, client review, parallel teams — is where most ambitious virtual production pipelines collapse.

Worldwright manages project structure, asset versioning, stage state (READY_FOR_BEEBLE, READY_FOR_POSTSHOT, READY_FOR_UNREAL), SceneDNA records, and re-entry support. Because every artifact is preserved with its parameters, any shot can be returned to any stage — re-train the splat with a higher budget, re-light the performer for a new edit, regenerate the world with a tweaked anomaly — without rebuilding the project. This is what makes Anomaly X a studio rather than a portfolio.
